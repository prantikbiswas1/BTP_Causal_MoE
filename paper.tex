\section{Causal Mixture-of-Experts: Semantic Step-Wise Expert Routing
for Efficient and Interpretable LLM
Reasoning}\label{causal-mixture-of-experts-semantic-step-wise-expert-routing-for-efficient-and-interpretable-llm-reasoning}

\textbf{Author:} Prantik Biswas \textbf{Institution:} Indian Institute
of Technology Patna \textbf{Degree Program:} B.Tech Project (BTP)
\textbf{Date:} April 2026

\begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{0.5pt}\end{center}

\subsection{Abstract}\label{abstract}

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable reasoning
capabilities, yet their inference procedure remains computationally
monolithic: every token is processed by the full model regardless of its
semantic role. We introduce the \textbf{Causal Mixture-of-Experts
(Causal-MoE)}, a novel architectural framework that performs
\emph{reasoning-step-level} sparse routing, dispatching distinct
semantic operations to dedicated domain-specific expert modules. Built
upon the Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct backbone, our architecture surgically
replaces standard Feed-Forward Network (FFN) layers at four strategic
depths with a \textbf{Sparse Gated MoE MLP}, containing four specialized
LoRA-based experts: \texttt{{[}MATH{]}}, \texttt{{[}LOGIC{]}},
\texttt{{[}COMMONSENSE{]}}, and \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}}. We train the
model on curated \emph{Atomic Reasoning Traces} and evaluate on two
widely-used benchmarks: \textbf{GSM8K} (math word problems) and
\textbf{CommonsenseQA} (knowledge-grounded multiple choice). Our V2
integrated model achieves \textbf{76.80\% accuracy on GSM8K} (a +8.72 pp
improvement over V1) while reducing estimated inference FLOPS by
\textbf{34\% below the base model} (2.81T vs 4.28T). Our V1 router-based
model achieves \textbf{83.05\% on CommonsenseQA}, exceeding the base
model. We demonstrate that semantic step-level routing enables a
fundamentally new trade-off: reasoning transparency, modular
specialization, and competitive accuracy---all within a single 7B
parameter model.

\subsection{1. Introduction}\label{introduction}

\subsubsection{1.1 The Problem: Compute-Reasoning
Displacement}\label{the-problem-compute-reasoning-displacement}

Modern auto-regressive language models generate output tokens
sequentially, with each forward pass consuming compute proportional to
the full parameter count. This is inefficient for multi-step reasoning
tasks, where different sub-problems (arithmetic, logical deduction,
world-knowledge retrieval) have fundamentally different computational
needs. The phenomenon we call \textbf{Compute-Reasoning Displacement}
describes this mismatch: the model spends the same FLOPs to generate a
filler token (``the answer is\ldots{}'') as it does to execute a complex
multi-operand arithmetic step.

This problem manifests in two empirically observable ways:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Latency Explosion}: Standard Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting
  adds hundreds of tokens per query. For GSM8K, the Qwen2.5-7B base
  model generates an average of \textbf{208.9 tokens} per answer --- a
  large fraction of which is redundant scaffolding.
\item
  \textbf{Reasoning Opacity}: A flat sequence of tokens provides no
  sub-task boundaries, making it difficult to attribute errors or inject
  corrections mid-trace.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{1.2 Our Approach: Semantic Step-level
Routing}\label{our-approach-semantic-step-level-routing}

We propose to partition the reasoning process into a sequence of
explicitly-typed steps, each handled by a specialized expert. Our
approach draws on two complementary ideas:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)}: Activate only a subset of
  parameters for any given input token or reasoning step, improving
  efficiency {[}Fedus et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024{]}.
\item
  \textbf{Structured Chain-of-Thought}: Tag intermediate reasoning steps
  with domain labels (e.g., \texttt{{[}MATH{]}}, \texttt{{[}LOGIC{]}}),
  enabling structured tracing and targeted specialization {[}Wei et al.,
  2022{]}.
\end{itemize}

The key novelty of Causal-MoE is that routing decisions operate at the
\textbf{semantic step level} rather than the token level. This alignment
with human cognitive sub-goals enables both efficiency (fewer tokens per
step) and interpretability (every step is attributed to a domain).

\subsubsection{1.3 Contributions}\label{contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  We introduce the \textbf{Causal MoE architecture}, which surgically
  injects sparse expert MLP layers into a pre-trained 7B model with zero
  disruption to the attention mechanism.
\item
  We define the \textbf{Atomic Reasoning Trace} training format, where
  each output token range is domain-tagged, teaching the model to
  internalize expert routing.
\item
  We demonstrate two inference paradigms: \textbf{Exploded (V1)}
  multi-call routing and \textbf{Collapsed (V2)} single-pass integrated
  routing, with distinct trade-off profiles.
\item
  We achieve a \textbf{34\% FLOP reduction} relative to the base model
  on GSM8K in V2, while maintaining 76.80\% accuracy --- validating our
  ``Compressed CoT'' hypothesis.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{2.2 Chain-of-Thought and Structured
Reasoning}\label{chain-of-thought-and-structured-reasoning}

CoT prompting {[}Wei et al., 2022{]} improves reasoning by encouraging
the model to generate intermediate steps before the final answer.
However, CoT latency scales linearly with trace length, which is
unbounded. Least-to-Most prompting, Program-of-Thought, and other
structured approaches attempt to impose structure, but still use a
single monolithic model for all operations.

Our work is most related to \textbf{PAL (Program-Aided LLMs)} {[}Gao et
al., 2023{]} and \textbf{Tool-Augmented LMs} in spirit: we introduce a
structured execution trace, but we do so \emph{without external tools},
instead routing to specialized parameter-space experts \emph{within} the
model.

\subsubsection{2.3 Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
(PEFT)}\label{parameter-efficient-fine-tuning-peft}

We use LoRA {[}Hu et al., 2022{]} to train our domain experts
efficiently. LoRA introduces low-rank decomposition matrices
\(\Delta W = BA\) (where \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times r}\),
\(A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times k}\), \(r \ll \min(d,k)\)) alongside frozen
pre-trained weights, enabling expert specialization without catastrophic
forgetting.

\subsection{3. The Causal-MoE
Architecture}\label{the-causal-moe-architecture}

\subsubsection{3.1 Overview}\label{overview}

The Causal-MoE builds upon the \textbf{Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct} backbone ---
a 28-layer decoder-only Transformer with hidden size \(d = 3584\) and
intermediate size \(d_{FFN} = 18944\). We leave the attention mechanism
entirely intact and perform ``architectural surgery'' solely on the MLP
sub-layers at four selected depths.

\textbf{Surgical Injection Points}: Layers
\(\mathcal{L} = \{6, 12, 18, 24\}\).

At these layers, the standard SwiGLU FFN is replaced by the
\textbf{CausalMoEMLP} module. All other layers remain frozen or are
fine-tuned via LoRA applied to attention projections
\texttt{\{q\_proj,\ k\_proj,\ v\_proj,\ o\_proj\}}.

\textbf{Figure 1} below illustrates the full V1 (Exploded) Causal-MoE
system: the high-level Qwen2.5-1.5B router, the domain-specific LoRA
pool, the autoregressive multi-tag inference loop, and a side-by-side
comparison with the Mixtral MoE philosophy. \textbf{Figure 2} shows the
V2 (Collapsed) architecture, highlighting the 28-layer backbone spine,
the surgically injected MoE cells at layers 6/12/18/24, LoRA discs
plugging into each layer, and the sparse activation flow within a single
MoE cell at Layer 6.

\subsubsection{3.2 The CausalMoEMLP
Module}\label{the-causalmoemlp-module}

Each injected layer contains:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{A Gating Network}: A linear projection
  \(G(\mathbf{h}) = \mathbf{h} W_{gate} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_e}\), where
  \(N_e = 4\) is the number of experts.
\item
  \textbf{\(N_e\) Expert FFNs}: Each expert \(i\) implements a reduced
  SwiGLU:
\end{enumerate}

\[\text{Expert}_i(\mathbf{h}) = \left(\sigma(\mathbf{h} W_{gate}^{(i)}) \odot \mathbf{h} W_{up}^{(i)}\right) W_{down}^{(i)}\]

where \(W_{gate}^{(i)}, W_{up}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_{exp}}\)
and \(W_{down}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{exp} \times d}\), with
\(d_{exp} = \lfloor d_{FFN} \times \rho \rfloor\) and \textbf{reduction
factor} \(\rho = 0.5\).

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\setcounter{enumi}{2}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Sparse Top-k Routing}: For a token representation
  \(\mathbf{h}\), the router selects the top-\(k = 2\) experts by
  normalized weight:
\end{enumerate}

\[\mathbf{p} = \text{Softmax}(G(\mathbf{h})), \quad (j_1, j_2) = \text{top-}k(\mathbf{p})\]

\[\tilde{\mathbf{p}} = \frac{\mathbf{p}_{j}}{\mathbf{p}_{j_1} + \mathbf{p}_{j_2}}, \quad \text{for } j \in \{j_1, j_2\}\]

\[\text{MoE}(\mathbf{h}) = \sum_{j \in \{j_1, j_2\}} \tilde{p}_j \cdot \text{Expert}_j(\mathbf{h})\]

\subsubsection{3.3 Expert Initialization: Interleaved Weight
Slicing}\label{expert-initialization-interleaved-weight-slicing}

Experts are not initialized randomly. We use \textbf{Interleaved Weight
Slicing} to partition the pre-trained FFN weights across experts,
preserving the learned representations:

Let \(\mathcal{E} = \{even\_indices, odd\_indices\}\) denote the
interleaved index partitions of \([0, d_{FFN})\). Expert \(i\) is
assigned slice \(S_i \subset \{0, \ldots, d_{FFN}-1\}\),
\(|S_i| = d_{exp}\).

\[W_{gate}^{(i)} = W_{gate}^{base}[S_i, :], \quad W_{up}^{(i)} = W_{up}^{base}[S_i, :], \quad W_{down}^{(i)} = W_{down}^{base}[:, S_i]\]

This ensures that each expert begins training with a meaningful
sub-capacity of the original FFN, rather than noise. Even-indexed
experts (\(i \in \{0, 2\}\)) are initialized from even-index neurons;
odd-indexed experts (\(i \in \{1, 3\}\)) from odd-index neurons.

\textbf{Complexity Analysis}: With \(N_e = 4\), \(\rho = 0.5\), and
top-\(k = 2\), each MoE layer activates \(2 \times 0.5 = 1.0\)
expert-equivalents --- matching the original FFN compute. The efficiency
gain comes from the reduction in generated tokens (and therefore total
forward passes) at inference time.

\subsection{4. Training Methodology}\label{training-methodology}

\subsubsection{4.1 The Atomic Reasoning Trace
Format}\label{the-atomic-reasoning-trace-format}

We define an \textbf{Atomic Reasoning Trace} as a structured completion
where every sub-step is prefixed with a domain tag:

\begin{verbatim}
### Instruction:
Analyze the following problem using ATOMIC reasoning tags: [MATH], [LOGIC], [COMMONSENSE].
ALWAYS conclude with [VERIFY] #### [answer].

### Input:
{question}

### Response:
[MATH] 16 - 3 - 4 = 9
[MATH] 9 * $2 = $18
[VERIFY] #### 18
\end{verbatim}

This format serves dual purpose: 1. \textbf{Expert Signal}: The model
learns to associate domain tags with the corresponding MoE expert
activation pattern. 2. \textbf{Trace Compression}: By constraining
individual steps to atomic operations, the total generated token count
is dramatically reduced compared to verbose CoT.

\subsubsection{4.2 Prompt Engineering and Dataset
Curation}\label{prompt-engineering-and-dataset-curation}

Training data consists of curated traces from GSM8K and CommonsenseQA: -
\textbf{Math traces}: Each arithmetic step is extracted and prefixed
with \texttt{{[}MATH{]}}. Verbal filler (``therefore'', ``thus'',
``hence'', ``so'') is programmatically removed by the
\texttt{clean\_math()} function. - \textbf{Logic traces}: Deductive
reasoning steps are prefixed with \texttt{{[}LOGIC{]}}. -
\textbf{Commonsense traces}: Heuristic/world-knowledge steps are
prefixed with \texttt{{[}COMMONSENSE{]}}. - \textbf{Verify steps}: Final
answer extraction in standard
\texttt{\#\#\#\#\ \textless{}value\textgreater{}} format is prefixed
with \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}}.

Label masking is applied such that cross-entropy loss is computed
\textbf{only over the response tokens} (prompt tokens receive label
\(= -100\)), preventing the model from learning to re-generate the
instruction.

\subsection{5. Inference Paradigms: V1 vs
V2}\label{inference-paradigms-v1-vs-v2}

We evaluate two distinct inference strategies representing different
trade-off points on the accuracy-efficiency curve. The two paradigms are
visualised in \textbf{Figure 1} (V1) and \textbf{Figure 2} (V2)
respectively. The core difference is the locus of the routing decision:
external multi-call orchestration (V1) versus internalized single-pass
trace generation (V2).

\subsubsection{5.1 V1: Exploded Multi-Call
Routing}\label{v1-exploded-multi-call-routing}

In V1, an external \textbf{Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct} model acts as a
``Causal Router.'' At each reasoning step:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The router receives the full conversation history and predicts the
  next expert tag (e.g., \texttt{{[}MATH{]}}).
\item
  The corresponding expert LoRA is loaded and generates a single atomic
  step.
\item
  The output is appended to history. The process repeats until
  \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}} is selected.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Advantage}: Maximum expert isolation. The router can consult the
entire context before each decision. \textbf{Disadvantage}: \(O(S)\)
model calls (where \(S\) is the number of steps), leading to
\(O(S \times P)\) prefill operations --- high amortized FLOP cost.

Average steps: \(S_{CSQA} = 5.1\), \(S_{GSM8K} = 5.3\). Even though
per-step token counts are low, the repeated prefilling of the router and
expert creates a non-trivial total FLOP overhead.

\subsubsection{5.2 V2: Collapsed Single-Pass
Integration}\label{v2-collapsed-single-pass-integration}

In V2, the expert routing is internalized into the backbone. A single
forward pass generates the full Atomic Reasoning Trace in one call,
including all domain-tagged steps and the final \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}}
answer.

The model learns from training that: - Tokens following
\texttt{{[}MATH{]}} tags should activate MoE layers that favor
arithmetic expert weights. - Tokens following \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}}
should activate the pruning/formatting expert.

This ``collapse'' eliminates the multi-call overhead entirely, giving
\(O(1)\) forward passes per query.

\textbf{Average Steps in V2}: \(S_{GSM8K} = 1.0\) (single-pass
generation).

\subsubsection{5.3 FLOP Budget Estimation}\label{flop-budget-estimation}

We estimate FLOPs using the standard approximation:

\[\text{FLOP}(model, tokens) \approx 2 \times P_{active} \times T_{total}\]

where \(P_{active} = 6.67 \times 10^9\) (post-sparsity active parameters
for V2) and \(T_{total}\) is the sum of input and output tokens per
query.

For the V1 router pipeline:

\[\text{FLOP}_{V1} = \sum_{s=1}^{S} \left[ 2 \times P_{router} \times T_{router,s} + 2 \times P_{expert} \times T_{expert,s} \right]\]

where \(P_{router} = 1.5 \times 10^9\) and
\(P_{expert} = 7.0 \times 10^9\).

\subsection{7. Qualitative Analysis}\label{qualitative-analysis}

\subsubsection{7.1 Success Case: CommonsenseQA
(V1)}\label{success-case-commonsenseqa-v1}

\textbf{Question}: ``Banks are usually quite secure. What type of door
might they have to make it even more so?''

\textbf{Causal MoE V1 Trace}:

\begin{verbatim}
[COMMONSENSE] Revolving door allows circular entry, multiple openings
[COMMONSENSE] Revolving doors prevent tailgating, unauthorized entry
[LOGIC] Revolving doors are security features
[LOGIC] Banks require strict security measures
[VERIFY] #### A
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Ground Truth}: A (Revolving door). ✅

\textbf{Analysis}: The trace demonstrates the power of multi-domain
expert mixing. The \texttt{{[}COMMONSENSE{]}} expert first retrieves
factual knowledge about revolving doors; the \texttt{{[}LOGIC{]}} expert
then deduces the security implication; \texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}} maps to the
answer choice. The final trace is only 5 compact steps, compared to the
base model's \textasciitilde197 tokens of verbose prose.

\subsubsection{7.2 Progress Case: GSM8K
(V2)}\label{progress-case-gsm8k-v2}

\textbf{Question}: Josh buys a house for \$80,000, puts in \$50,000 in
repairs, increasing its value by 150\%. What is his profit?

\emph{Correct solution}: New value = \(80,000 \times 2.5 = \$200,000\).
Profit = \(200,000 - 130,000 = \$70,000\).

\textbf{Causal MoE V2 Trace}:

\begin{verbatim}
[MATH] 80,000 + 50,000 = 130,000
[MATH] 130,000 * 1.50 = 195,000
[MATH] 195,000 - 130,000 = 65,000
[VERIFY] #### 65000
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Ground Truth}: 70,000. ❌ (off by \$5,000)

\textbf{Error Analysis}: The model correctly identifies the total cost
(\$130k) and the subtraction. The error is \textbf{semantic}: the 150\%
increase is applied to the \emph{total investment} (\$130k) rather than
the \emph{original purchase price} (\$80k). This is a \emph{constraint
binding error} --- the model loses reference to the original anchor
value during a multi-step computation. Notably, this is a semantic
reasoning error rather than an arithmetic one, representing a
significant qualitative improvement over V1 which would typically
produce structurally incoherent traces.

\textbf{Base Model Trace} (192 words): Produces the same semantic error
in 280+ tokens, with redundant explanatory prose and without the
interpretable step-level attribution.

\subsection{8. Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsubsection{8.1 Why the Architecture
Works}\label{why-the-architecture-works}

The success of Causal-MoE stems from a synergy between three properties:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Specialization via PEFT}: LoRA adapters allow domain-specific
  weight deltas over the pre-trained parameters. The pre-trained base
  provides strong general priors; the LoRA delta carves out a
  specialized sub-manifold in parameter space for each domain.
\item
  \textbf{Interleaved Initialization}: By partitioning pre-trained FFN
  neurons (rather than random initialization), each expert begins with a
  coherent partial representation of the original FFN, avoiding the
  ``cold start'' instability of random expert initialization.
\item
  \textbf{Semantic Trace Training}: Training on Atomic Reasoning Traces
  creates an implicit alignment between the textual domain tags
  (\texttt{{[}MATH{]}}, etc.) and the MoE routing decisions. After
  training, the model's internal gating signal co-varies with the
  semantic domain of the current step.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{8.2 The V1 vs V2 Trade-off}\label{the-v1-vs-v2-trade-off}

The V1 / V2 duality represents a fundamental trade-off in the design of
reasoning models:

computational budget are the primary constraints --- the typical
production scenario.

\subsubsection{8.3 Comparison with
Mixtral}\label{comparison-with-mixtral}

Unlike Mixtral's token-level routing, our trace-level routing aligns
with the human cognitive decomposition of problems. Mixtral routes
\textasciitilde2 of 8 FFN experts per token (\textasciitilde8×7B total,
\textasciitilde12B active parameters). In contrast, our model routes
\textasciitilde2 of 4 semantic experts across only 4 layers of a 7B
model. The critical difference is that our routing carries
\emph{semantic meaning}: the choice of \texttt{{[}MATH{]}} vs
\texttt{{[}COMMONSENSE{]}} is interpretable, attributable, and trainable
in a targeted manner.

\subsubsection{9.2 Qualitative Impact}\label{qualitative-impact}

Beyond the numerical results, the architecture delivers three
non-quantifiable but practically significant benefits:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Error Attribution}: Because each step is domain-tagged, model
  errors can be attributed to a specific expert (e.g., ``the MATH expert
  missed the variable binding''). This enables targeted fine-tuning
  rather than full model retraining.
\item
  \textbf{Modular Extensibility}: Adding a new reasoning domain (e.g.,
  \texttt{{[}CODE{]}} for programming tasks) requires only a new LoRA
  expert, without disturbing existing experts.
\item
  \textbf{Curriculum Learning Compatibility}: Each expert can be trained
  independently on its domain before joint MoE training, allowing a
  structured curriculum.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{10. Limitations}\label{limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{GSM8K Gap vs Base}: Even V2 (76.80\%) trails the base model
  (88.86\%) on GSM8K, a 12 pp gap. This reflects the inherent difficulty
  of compressing reasoning: the base model uses verbose but effective
  step-by-step narration; our model sacrifices some reliability for
  compactness.
\item
  \textbf{CSQA Regression in V2}: The single-pass paradigm loses the
  ``breadth-first exploration'' that V1's multi-call loop enables.
  Open-ended commonsense queries benefit from contextual multi-hop
  retrieval which the collapsed format does not support.
\item
  \textbf{Constraint Binding}: The model struggles to propagate anchor
  values (e.g., the base price in a percentage problem) across a
  multi-step trace, suggesting the need for explicit scratchpad/memory
  mechanisms.
\item
  \textbf{FLOP Estimation Approximate}: Our FLOP estimates use
  \(2 \times P \times T\), which does not account for attention
  complexity (\(O(T^2 \cdot d)\)) or activation memory overheads.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{11. Future Work}\label{future-work}

\subsubsection{11.1 Hybrid V1/V2 (Adaptive Routing
Depth)}\label{hybrid-v1v2-adaptive-routing-depth}

A unified model could dynamically switch between multi-call and
single-pass paradigms based on query complexity, estimated using a
lightweight classifier on the input.

\subsubsection{11.2 Self-Correcting VERIFY Expert
(Verify+)}\label{self-correcting-verify-expert-verify}

Implement a beam-search or Best-of-N strategy within the
\texttt{{[}VERIFY{]}} expert, generating multiple candidate answers and
selecting the one that satisfies constraint-checking rules (e.g., ``the
answer must be less than X'').

\subsubsection{11.3 Cross-Expert Memory (Global Context
Layer)}\label{cross-expert-memory-global-context-layer}

Add a lightweight key-value side-channel that persists global anchors
(numerical constants, entity references) across expert steps, directly
addressing the constraint binding failure mode.

\subsubsection{11.4 Scaling to More Experts and Broader
Domains}\label{scaling-to-more-experts-and-broader-domains}

Extend the expert pool to include \texttt{{[}CODE{]}},
\texttt{{[}SPATIAL{]}}, \texttt{{[}TEMPORAL{]}} experts and evaluate on
benchmarks like MATH, HumanEval, and ARC-Challenge.

\subsection{12. Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

We present the \textbf{Causal Mixture-of-Experts (Causal-MoE)}
framework, which operationalizes \emph{semantic step-level routing} as
an alternative to the standard flat auto-regressive generation paradigm.
By surgically injecting sparse expert MLP layers at four depths of
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and training on Atomic Reasoning Traces, we achieve:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  A \textbf{31.7\% improvement in Reasoning Density} over the base model
  (V2 vs Base on GSM8K).
\item
  A \textbf{34\% reduction in inference FLOPs} below the unmodified base
  model on GSM8K.
\item
  A \textbf{+0.99 pp accuracy gain on CommonsenseQA} (V1 vs Base),
  demonstrating that multi-step expert mixing benefits open-domain
  reasoning.
\item
  A \textbf{+8.72 pp GSM8K accuracy recovery} from V1 to V2, validating
  the integrated trace learning approach.
\end{itemize}

The architecture demonstrates that explicit semantic routing ---
aligning the model's computational sub-modules with human-interpretable
reasoning types --- is a viable path toward LLMs that are simultaneously
more efficient, more transparent, and more correct.
